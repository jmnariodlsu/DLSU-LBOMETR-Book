2.1 Course Information

2.1.1 General Course Description

This course introduces Economics majors to more advanced commands and techniques used in the econometric software package R, commonly used in empirical research.

2.1.2 Specific Course Description

This course familiarizes Economics majors with advanced techniques in R for empirical research. Students will gather and analyze their own real-world data to investigate a specific economic problem, applying econometric methods and presenting their findings in a final project.

2.2 Learning Goals and Outcomes

2.2.1 Expected Lasallian Graduate Attributes (ELGAs)

  1. Culturally Sensitive, Critical, and Creative Thinkers
  2. Effective Communicator
  3. Reflective Lifelong Learner and Competitive Self-Nurturer
  4. Engaged and Service-Driven Citizen
  5. Virtue-Guided Decision-Maker
  6. Technically Proficient Professional

2.2.2 Programme Learning Outcomes (PLOs)

2.2.2.1 Pre-Major (Theorising)

  1. Describe key facts and issues about the Philippine and ASEAN+X economies, and explain how basic indicators are formulated
  2. Use tables, graphs, algebra, and calculus as primary analytical tools
  3. Transition from high school work and habits to become avid readers, clear writers, and quick synthesisers of college-level material
  4. Consistently exhibit self-regulation, valuing other people’s time

2.2.2.2 Major (Solving)

  1. Analyse the workings and history of key markets and institutions using current economic models
  2. Implement the scientific method on economic problems: developing hypotheses, envisioning data requirements, analysing with mathematical and statistical tools
  3. Adapt to new situations and respond maturely to setbacks
  4. Consistently work well with others, participate in multidisciplinary discussions with an open mind and respect for others’ views

2.2.2.3 Capstone (Translating)

  1. Evaluate market policies and decisions using key economic principles, particularly opportunity-cost, marginal, and positive-sum thinking
  2. Create short-form and long-form research that analyses real-world issues and incorporates knowledge from other disciplines
  3. Communicate sensibly and sensitively with different audiences
  4. Synthesise and exhibit what it means to be a Lasallian economist, to bridge faith and scholarship

2.2.3 Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs)

2.2.3.1 Knowledge

  • Distinguish a theoretical economic model from a statistical econometric model
  • Use the R software package in estimating advanced econometric models
  • Learn advanced econometric models to explore new methods of research

2.2.3.2 Skills

  • Apply numerical and statistical techniques in economic analysis
  • Use statistical concepts as a language in economic discourse
  • Confidently write script files for economic analysis

2.2.3.3 Behavior/Attitude

  • Demonstrate transparency and academic integrity in handling data analysis
  • Construct more complex programs from basic commands learned in class

2.7 Class Policies

2.7.1 1. Attendance

Attendance will be checked at random times during class and counts for 5% of the final grade.

  • For each unexcused absence, 0.6 points will be deducted.
  • Excused absences (with valid documentation) will not incur deductions (see Appendix).

2.7.2 2. Course Materials

All materials related to LBOMETR will be made available through AnimoSpace.

Improper use, reproduction, or distribution of LBOMETR learning and teaching materials constitutes a violation of academic integrity and will be reported to the Discipline Office.

2.7.3 3. Grading Notes

  • Achieving a perfect score (100%) in this course is extremely difficult.
  • Even excellent students typically achieve a maximum of around 95%.
  • Grades are strictly based on demonstrated mastery, output quality, and adherence to course rubrics.

2.7.4 4. Incentives and Extra Credit

No general incentives will be provided in this class. However, an optional extra credit assignment is available:

  • Students may submit a 5-minute vlog explaining a chosen lab econometrics method and how they intend to apply it in real-world research.
  • Successful submission will earn an additional 0.3 points added to the final raw grade.
  • The vlog must be submitted by email only, no later than August 8, 21:00.
  • Details are outlined in the Other Requirements or Activities section.

2.7.5 5. Computer Practicals

a. Group Participation - Computer Practicals must be completed in groups. - Every group member is required to participate and contribute at least once during class activities. - Non-participation may result in deductions from the Group Participation grade.

b. Weekly Submission - Practicals are not graded weekly. - All completed practicals must be compiled and submitted as an appendix to the Data Story Archive.

c. Midway Check - A midway practical check will ensure steady progress. - Each group must show evidence that at least 50% of practicals have been completed and stored (e.g., Quarto files, datasets, meeting notes). - Incomplete work may result in deductions from both the Group Participation grade and other assessments.

2.7.6 6. Groupings and Peer Evaluation

a. Group Formation - Groups (4–5 members) will be randomly assigned based on a survey conducted in the first meeting.

b. Peer Evaluation Two evaluations will be conducted:

  • Midway Evaluation (Week 7): Peer assessment via anonymous Google Form.
    • Students scoring below the class median will receive a warning email.
  • Final Evaluation (after project submission):
    • Students scoring below the median in either or both evaluations will face deductions from their Group Participation and possibly other assessment grades.

2.7.7 7. Extensions

  • No extensions will be granted.
  • There are only two major deadlines in this course—students are expected to manage their time accordingly.

2.7.8 8. Use of AI Tools

A full AI Use Policy is provided in the Appendix. In brief:

  • AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT) are allowed only for coding assistance (e.g., debugging).
  • Students must acknowledge AI use in their submissions.
  • Students must critically review and verify all AI outputs.
  • AI-generated content or unchecked code must not be submitted as final work.

2.7.9 9. Consultations

a. Data Story Topic Submission - Submit topic via email by May 23, 2025, at 21:00. - Subject line format: LBOMETR [Section] – Group No. – Data Story Topic. - In the email body: describe the topic, explain why it was chosen, and list expected data sources. - Topic changes must be made in the same email thread. - No changes allowed after Week 6. - Unauthorized changes beyond Week 6 will result in a 50% deduction from the Data Story and related grades.

b. Mock Presentations - No live mock presentations due to a conference from June 23–27. - Groups must submit recorded mock presentations: - Monday groups: by Friday, June 20, 2025, at 21:00. - Thursday groups: by Tuesday, June 24, 2025, at 21:00. - Recordings must be clear, professional, and reflect the intended structure of the final presentation.

c. Data Story Archive Consultations - After mock submissions, groups are encouraged to book 15-minute consultations to improve their archives. - Booking link will be provided.

d. Other Consultations - For other concerns, email the professor. - If there’s no reply within 3 days, send a polite follow-up email.

e. Final Presentation Venue - A HyFlex classroom will be used for the final presentation. - Groups must prepare to present to both in-person and online audiences.
